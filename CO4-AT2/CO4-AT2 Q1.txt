1. Warehouse delivery route optimization(greedy strategy)
A logistics company operates a fleet of delivery vehicles that must deliver packages to multiple locations. Each route has a diffrent delvery cost and distance.
The company wants to minimize the total transportaion cost while ensuring all location are connected.
Task:
1.model the problem as a graph and identify an apporiate greedy alforithm to solve it
2.explain how the algorithm selects routes to achive minimum cost.
3.Analyze the time complexity of the chosen algorithm
4. Discuss the advantage and the limitation of using greedy approach for this problem.
expected concepts: minimum spanning tree, krusal algorithm, prims algorithm, greedy strategy.

Answer:
1. Graph Modeling & Algorithm
Vertices ( V ): Delivery locations.
Edges (E): Available routes between locations.
Weights (W): The monetary cost to traverse each route.
Target: Find the Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) to connect all points at the absolute lowest cost.
Chosen Greedy Algorithm: Kruskal’s Algorithm (ideal for mapping out infrastructure networks efficiently).
2. How the Greedy Strategy WorksSort: List all available routes and sort them in ascending order by cost (cheapest first).
Select: Iterate through the sorted routes and pick the cheapest one available.
Check (Cycle Avoidance): Using a Union-Find data structure, check if adding the route creates a loop. If it connects two previously disconnected areas, keep it. If it forms a closed loop, discard it.
Stop: Terminate as soon as V - 1 routes are chosen, successfully connecting every location.
3. Time ComplexitySorting edges: O(E \log E)
Disjoint-Set operations (Cycle detection): O(E . alpha(V))
Total Complexity: O(E log V) (where E is the number of routes and V is the number of locations). This is highly efficient and scales well.
4. Pros & Cons of the Greedy Approach
Advantages:
Guaranteed Best Network Layout: Mathematically proven to find the global minimum cost layout for a connected network.
Fast and Scalable: Executes rapidly even for large logistics networks with thousands of nodes.
Limitations:
Ignores Reality Constraints: Does not account for delivery deadlines, traffic conditions, or vehicle load capacities.
Not a Single Continuous Tour: It creates a branching tree structure. If a single truck needs to complete a round-trip loop visiting multiple stops (like a mail carrier), this problem becomes the Traveling Salesperson Problem (TSP), where a simple greedy approach fails to find the best solution.
